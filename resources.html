Township 101

Plain language guides to local government, public budgets, and the civic tools that already belong to you. No jargon. No assumptions. Just the actual information.

Why This Page Exists

Your government keeps records.
Most people don't know where to find them.

Every dollar your township government spends is a public record. Every budget is filed with the state. Every audit is available online. The cemetery maintenance decisions, the assistance fund balance, the property management costs — all of it is there.

Most residents have never seen any of it. Not because they don't care, but because nobody told them it existed or explained how to read it when they found it.

This page is an attempt to change that. It will grow over time. Check back as we add more guides, more documents, and more plain language explanations of how your local government actually works.

What Civic Power Actually Means

Civic power is not something a politician gives you. It is not something you earn by knowing the right people or having the right education. It is something you already have because you live here, pay taxes here, and have a legal stake in every decision made in your name by people you may have never heard of in rooms you may not know exist.

The reason it does not feel that way is not because the power is missing. It is because the tools for using it are buried under language and process that was never designed to be easy to find.

Here is what civic power looks like at the township level in plain terms.

  • 1You have the right to attend every Township Board meeting. They are public. No invitation required.
  • 2You have the right to speak at public meetings during comment periods. Three minutes is typically the limit but your voice is on the record.
  • 3You have the right to request any public document including the township budget, the assistance fund records, and meeting minutes. This is not a favor. It is the law.
  • 4You have the right to apply for township assistance if you are in financial crisis. The program exists for exactly this and you do not have to be destitute to qualify.
  • 5You have the right to vote for or run for the trustee office. The filing deadline, the process, and the requirements are all public information.

The bottom line: The systems that govern your daily life were built with public input mechanisms. Most people never use them because nobody explained they existed. Now you know.

How to Read a Township Budget
Without a Finance Degree

Finding and Understanding the Union Township Budget

Every Indiana township is required by law to submit an annual budget to the Indiana Department of Local Government Finance. That budget is a public record and it is available to anyone online at no cost. Here is exactly how to find it and what you are looking at when you do.

Step by Step: Finding the Budget

  • 1Go to gateway.ifionline.org — this is Indiana's official public financial data portal.
  • 2Click on Report Builder in the navigation menu.
  • 3Under Report Type select Budget.
  • 4Under County select Porter.
  • 5Under Unit select Union Township.
  • 6Select the year you want to review and click View Report.
  • 7You now have the actual adopted budget for Union Township. Print it, save it, read it.

Direct link: gateway.ifionline.org/report_builder — bookmark this. It works for every local government in Indiana, not just Union Township. You can look up any county, city, school district, or township budget the same way.

What You Are Looking At

General Fund

The main operating budget. This covers the day to day costs of running the township office including salaries, supplies, and administrative expenses.

Township Assistance Fund

This is the emergency assistance budget — the money available to help residents with housing, utilities, food, and medical costs in a crisis. The size of this fund matters directly to your neighbors who need it.

Fire Protection Fund

Funds the township's fire protection contract or services. This is how fire coverage is paid for in areas without a municipal fire department.

Cemetery Fund

Funds maintenance of township cemeteries including abandoned burial grounds the trustee is legally responsible for preserving.

Levy

The total amount of property tax the township is authorized to collect. This is set through the state budget process and limits how much the township can spend.

Appropriation

The amount formally approved to be spent from a fund. Just because money is appropriated does not mean it all gets spent — but it cannot be spent without the appropriation.

Encumbrance

Money that has been committed to a purchase or contract but not yet paid out. It shows up as reserved in the budget.

Cash Balance

What is actually in the fund at a given point in time. A healthy township keeps a reasonable cash balance so it can respond to needs without waiting for the next tax distribution.

What to look for: Compare the Township Assistance Fund appropriation to what was actually spent. A large gap between those two numbers tells you something about whether residents who qualify are actually being connected to help. That is a question worth asking your trustee.

Public Documents

Union Township Public Records
and Where to Find Them

These are the real documents. All of them are public. All of them are free to access. You do not need to file a request or hire anyone to get them. You just need to know where to look.

Indiana Gateway

Township Budget Reports

Annual adopted budgets for Union Township going back multiple years. Select Porter County then Union Township in the Report Builder.

gateway.ifionline.org

Indiana Gateway

Annual Financial Reports

Actual revenues and expenditures reported each year. Shows what was spent versus what was budgeted — often more revealing than the budget itself.

gateway.ifionline.org

Indiana State Board of Accounts

Township Audit Reports

Independent audits of township finances conducted by the state. Search for Union Township Porter County. The most recent audit covering 2019 to 2022 found no reportable instances of noncompliance.

in.gov/sboa/audit-reports

Indiana DLGF

Budget Notices and Hearings

Public hearing dates and adopted budget notices for local government units. This is where you find out when budget hearings are scheduled so you can attend and comment.

budgetnotices.in.gov

Porter County

County Budget and Finance

Porter County's official budget and finance page with links to county level financial documents and settlement reports showing how tax distributions flow to townships.

portercountyin.gov

Indiana Gateway

Full Financial Portal

The main Indiana Gateway portal. From here you can access budget data, annual financial reports, debt disclosures, and capital asset information for every local government unit in Indiana.

gateway.ifionline.org
